Google Trips Plus

See more. Argue less.

Project details

Duration: 1 week during the intensive 3 week UX Engineering course

Group: 5 people

Used tools

Lean Canvas, User Story Mapping

Testing through poster sessions, A/B testing, Think aloud

Microinteractions

Figma, Illustrator

Requirement (purpose?)

Create a minimum viable prototype for a system that in some way act, or are perceived to act, "intelligently" by its users.

Brainstorming result

The Idea

Improve the Google Trips system by adding an intelligent feature for group management.

Why this idea?

Context

Organizing a group trip is a complex problem, because different people have different interests, which leads to misunderstandings within the group. During group travelling, it is not easy to find the attractions which fit everyoneâ€™s taste. There is already an app (Google trips) that is collecting data and giving recommendations based on that, but they cannot manage groups, just individuals.

Goal

Solution

An adaptive system which is connected to Google Trips and in this way with Google accounts. Every member who accepts the invitation will be analyzed and the recommended places will be based on clustering of individual preferences.

Process

1st step

We started out by creating the Lean Canvas of the idea, focusing on the problem, customer segment, unique value proposition and solution.

2nd step

Definition of the user goals, activities and tasks using User Story Mapping. The USM was intensively tested with users and refined using the received feedback.

3rd step

Validation of the problem through a survey concerning travelling habits and multiple poster sessions.

We started with a slightly different idea of getting personalised recommendations based on travelling history, liked pictures, pages, people on social media. After the first poster session we pivoted the concept because the audience was not convinced.

What can be changed?

Suggestions for improvements

A more accessible, recognisable and memorable check-out system would be thoroughly advantageous, particularly on busses. The high proportion of users from the survey that reported they have failed to check out several times, along with correlation with the users who have not transferred properly on their journeys, suggests there is room for improvement with the check-in/out posts. The exact specification for this improvement would need investigating, but the findings from this research show this could be an effective change.

Another issue identified in the study, which also has prevalence in busses, was users running out of credit on their rejsekort without knowing. Couple this problem with the high correlation to users having difficulty finding a kiosk and you have a potential source of frustration. One solution to this could be to facilitate topping up via an application or at a kiosk on the bus itself. It is understandable that the development of such a solution could be costly, but it would provide a great deal of flexibility for users, especially those whose regular commute is from bus stop to bus stop and does encounter a kiosk. This issue could also be circumnavigated if the user could use an app to pay into the rejsekort system directly. They could top up their account on the app anytime and the app could even have additional functionality, such as: reminders to check in/out, reminders to transfer properly and integration with rejseplanen for journey planning.

These suggestions, as with any change to a system, would need further examination, prototyping and thorough implementation.

Remember for next time

Learnings

Test early, test everything. Don't spend too much time thinking about what is right or wrong. We lost a lot of time in the beginning for discussing all the details, when we could have just asked a bunch of people for their opinion and spend that time on adding the finishing touches to the MVP.
